Abstract: The systematic navigation of biocatalyst space is constrained by elusive structure-14 

activity rules and a lack of evolutionary history. Here, we present IMUSE, a strategy integrating 15 

machine learning with ultra-high-throughput screening. By screening millions of droplet-16 

encapsulated de novo enzymes, we generated massive synthetic sequence-structure datasets to 17 

train models that capture their complex fitness landscapes and biophysical principles. These 18 

models effectively guide functional exploration across both sequence and novel structure spaces. 19 

IMUSE identified synergistic triple mutations yielding ~5-fold activity improvements and 20 

discovered active second-generation designs with novel catalytic pockets, boosting the 21 

experimental success rate >4.9-fold (~30%). This work demonstrates how synthetic fitness 22 

landscapes bridge the data gap in de novo enzyme space, transforming stochastic search into 23 

deterministic navigation to unlock highly proficient biocatalysts beyond natural boundaries. 24 

Introduction 25 

Enzymatic catalysis is a kinetic process involving dynamic interaction between 26 

substrate and biocatalyst, yet evaluating and navigating the vast landscape of potential enzyme-27 

substrate pairings with reactivity remains challenging ( 1–4). Currently, the development of 28 

reactivity progresses along two primary strategies ( 5). The first involves the remodeling of 29 

natural scaffolds via directed evolution or machine learning models, which have successfully 30 

leveraged evolutionary information to map sequence-activity relationships (Fig. 1A) (6–8). 31 

Nevertheless, these approaches are intrinsically confined to the local fitness landscapes of a 32 

limited number of ancestral folds, thereby restricting the structural diversity required to pair 33 

with the vast chemical space of unnatural substrates ( 9). The second strategy, de novo design, 34 

seeks to overcome these limitations by constructing bespoke scaffolds tailored to specific 35 

chemical targets ( 10 –13 ). However, even with recent advances in artificial intelligence 36 

facilitating the generation of high-activity de novo enzymes inspired by native active sites, this 37 

vast, unexplored space lacks both evolutionary metadata and functional characterization, 38 

which thus impedes the establishment of effective strategies to systematically interrogate and 39 

navigate the functional scaffolds beyond known spaces ( 14 –17 ). 40

Despite these challenges, the recent success of machine learning in connecting natural 41 

enzyme sequence space with diverse chemical substrates provides a compelling logic for 42 

exploring unknown biocatalyst structure space ( 18 , 19 ). However, connecting the small 43 

molecule to de novo enzymes is inherently more complex than mapping substrates to a given 44 

enzyme family, as the former requires navigating a high-dimensional landscape defined by 45 

vast topological breadth and immense sequence diversity ( 20 , 9). We reasoned that bridging 46 

the data gap would empower data-driven strategies to extend their success from native 47 

sequence space to the rational navigation of de novo sequence space, and even further into 48 

structure space (Fig. 1B) (21 ). Once a sufficient dataset was obtained, then machine learning 49 

models could be built to navigate between these two landscapes and enable the discovery of 50 

biocatalyst in a substrate-oriented fashion. However, since nature provides no "evolutionary 51 

map" for non-natural substrates, de novo spaces remain data-impoverished. Furthermore, these 52 

de novo enzymes feature stable folds and mechanistically reasonable active sites, which would 53 

make their activities indistinguishable by data-driven approaches utilizing native datasets. To 54 

overcome this bottleneck, we proposed the generation of "synthetic fitness landscapes" through 55 

ultra-high-throughput screening. This strategy emulates the data-dense environment of natural 56 

evolution by producing massive, substrate-specific functional datasets, effectively creating a 57 

roadmap for de novo enzyme space analogous to natural sequence databases. 58 

In this study, we ask whether de novo enzyme space could be explored and exploited 59 

using machine learning. We developed a strategy called IMUSE (Integrating Machine-learning 60 

and Ultra-high-throughput Screening for Enzyme spaces exploration), which couples the 61 

sampling of low-energy scaffolds with ultra-high-throughput screening to generate data-dense 62 

functional repertoires spanning unexplored landscapes, thereby providing the empirical 63 

foundation for machine-learning model to identify functional patterns and navigate toward 64 

proficient de novo enzymes (Fig. 1C) . Isolating active designs within such massive libraries 65 

requires ultra-high-throughput screening methods, which can be achieved by generation of 66 

single droplet double emulsion (sdDE) ( 22 ). sdDE uses a microfluidic device to incorporate 67 

substrate and monoclonal variants that links genotype to catalytic phenotype at the single-cell 68 

level ( 23 ). To facilitate high-throughput screening, we selected zinc hydrolase as a model 69 

system and challenged it with synthetic fluorescein dibenzoate (FDBz) as the substrate. FDBz 70 

generates fluorescence upon hydrolysis, thereby serving as a quantitative readout of enzymatic 71 

activity. Ultimately, this platform yielded millions of droplets containing active de novo 72 

enzymes with diverse backbones, providing a foundational dataset to train a machine learning 73 

model for nominating active designs based on structural and sequence features. Applying the 74 

IMUSE, we bridged the data gap of de novo spaces and achieved substantial improvement in 75 

success rates in navigating both the sequence and structure landscapes, surpassing existing 76 

benchmarks. In sequence-space exploration, we identified variants whose catalytic efficiency 77 

was enhanced by approximately 5-fold through triple mutations that decouple kinetic 78 

constraints. Further, we constructed a second-generation library that samples a broader 79 

structural space, which yielded a higher proportion of active designs and whose characterized 80 

variants exhibited catalytic proficiency comparable to that of the native enzyme. 81 

Development of computational de novo enzyme design and ultra-high-throughput screening 82 

To explore the vast and previously uncharted structural landscape, we developed a 83 

physics-based generative approach for de novo enzyme design, centered on the precise 84 

configuration of the theozyme for the target chemical reaction (Fig. 2A) . Specifically, for zinc-85 

dependent catalysis, we systematically enumerated all possible histidine coordination 86

To overcome the bottleneck of functional verification for the generated massive de 107 

novo structures, we developed an ultra-high-throughput screening method by integrating 108 

single-droplet double emulsion with fluorescence-activated cell sorting (sdDE-FACS). This 109 

approach enabled the screening of millions of droplets within a single experimental run. 110 

Previous fluorescence-activated droplet sorting (FADS) methods have facilitated the screening 111 

of environmental metagenomes and engineered enzymes; however, while capable of assaying 112 

thousands of samples per second, their reliance on complex microfluidic setups remains a 113 

significant technical barrier to widespread application ( 29 –31 ). We developed a custom 114 

microfluidic platform that generates FACS-compatible double emulsion (DE) droplets ( fig. 115 

S3 ). Recognizing that prior DE systems often required additional lysis agents, which may 116 

complicate the discrimination of active enzymes, we constructed an arabinose-inducible 117 

periplasmic expression pathway in E. coli (32 ). This strategy compartmentalizes de novo 118 

enzymes within the periplasm to minimize interference from cytoplasmic proteins, thereby 119 

enabling accurate functional assessments (Fig. 2C) . To verify protein localization and retention 120 

within this compartment, we expressed EGFP in the periplasm and observed a good secretion 121 

of target without detectable leakage into the supernatant even with a prolonged incubation in 122 

reaction buffer (Fig. 2C) .123 

We designed a dual flow-focusing geometry chip system to generate highly 124 

monodisperse and stable DEs. In our system, the inner aqueous core co-encapsulates cells and 125 

substrates via an inlet tree with two widened channels, which minimizes cell-induced flow 126 

perturbations and enables immediate substrate-cell mixing, reducing the risk of product 127 

contamination across different designs ( fig. S1A ). This core is first emulsified in carrier oil to 128 

form water-in-oil (W/O) droplets, which are subsequently encapsulated within an outer 129 

aqueous phase to produce water-in-oil-in-water (W/O/W) DEs (Fig. 2C and fig. S3B) . By 130 

systematically optimizing fluid flow rates, we generated uniformly DE droplets (Fig. 2D, fig. 131 

S3D and fig. S4) . Our chip-design ensures that the DE droplets remain significantly smaller 132

To ensure high assay specificity and sensitivity within these microcompartments, we 136

monodisperse and intact throughout the sorting process (fig. 2H, I) .148 

To validate the compatibility of active de novo enzymes with this sdDE-FACS 149 

procedure, we ultimately assessed the stability of cell encapsulated DE droplets by evaluating 150 

leakage and cross-talk during prolonged incubation. Confocal imaging revealed a distinct 151 

fluorescence contrast between positive and negative droplets with negligible fluorescence 152 

leakage, while enzymatic assays of enrichments further confirmed that the platform reliably 153 

distinguished active designs (Fig. 2J, K) . Collectively, these results establish sdDE-FACS as 154 

a foundational platform for the systematic exploration of the de novo landscape. 155 

High-throughput screening and structure validation of Lib1 156 

With sdDE-FACS established, genes encoding the designed enzyme fragments were 157 

synthesized as oligo libraries and subsequently assembled into full-length genes using 158 

previously described method ( 35 ). The assembled library was then transformed into E. coli 159 

cells for functional screening at a throughput of thousands of droplets per second (Fig. 2C) .160 

Individual E. coli cells expressing unique enzyme sequence were co-compartmentalized with 161 

zinc sulfate (ZnSO 4) and fluorogenic substrate (FDBz) within microfluidic droplets. For the 162 

initial round, we applied permissive sorting gates to maximize the recovery of potential hits, 163 

followed sort rounds were performed under stringent conditions to enrich for highly active 164 

designs. The sorting pools were then subjected to deep-sequencing to identify potential hits. 165 

This workflow successfully isolated hundreds of active designs with diverse scaffolds from the 166 

vast background of non-functional sequences (Fig. 3A) . To assess whether existing 167 

computational metrics could navigate within data-impoverished spaces of these de novo 168 

enzymes, we evaluated the capability of physics-based metrics as well as deep learning-based 169 

scorers to distinguish between the active and inactive designs in Lib1 (Fig. 3B and fig. S6) .170 

Among those computational scores, the most dominant physical feature is radius of gyration 171 

(Rg) and fa_atr_per_res_noLIG, which gauges protein compactness and correlates with stable 172 

packing. The other dominant features are AlphaFold3 based, relating to the compatibility of 173 

the protein and the substrate (Fig. 3B) (36 ). Collectively, deep-sequencing analysis of the 174 

sorted populations identified a weak enrichment of certain features, though it also indicates 175 

that the current scoring metrics are still unable to reliably distinguish active de novo enzymes 176 

(37 ). 177

However, while all designs were active, they exhibited varied kinetic behaviors. A 186 

subset of designs, typified by variant 2-8, showed clear catalytic turnover but failed to reach 187 

saturation kinetics within the solubility limit of the substrate, suggesting a defect in substrate 188 

affinity ( KM) despite a functional catalytic center ( Fig. 3I ). Conversely, another group of 189 

designs displayed well-defined kinetic parameters for both substrate binding and turnover. To 190 

gain insight into the molecular features that impair the substrate affinity ( KM), we compared 191 

score metrics w/o substrate affinity. We found, for example, that scores representing higher 192 

structural stability (score_per_res_w/oLIG), lower ligand neighbor atomic density 193 

(ligand_neighbors, atom_neighbors), as well as proper flexibility (AF2_M3_rmsd) and proper 194 

binding energy (ddg_LIG), were favored in many proficient (determinable KM) designs, which 195 

suggests balance of the global stability and pocket flexibility are required for enzyme design 196 

(Fig. 3C and fig. S11) . To understand the atomic-level basis of these distinct catalytic profiles, 197 

we solved the X-ray crystal structures of representative designs. The solved structures 198 

exhibited remarkable agreement with the computational models, with Cα root-mean-square 199 

deviations (RMSD) as low as 0.76 Å for design 2-8 and 1.06 Å for design 11-7 (calculated 200 

over 120 residues), thereby validating the high fidelity of the global backbone generated by 201 

our physicochemical-based generation method (Fig. 3D, G and table S5) .202 

The structure of 2-8 offered mechanistic insights into its decoupled affinity and 203 

catalysis. Within the active site of 2-8, an acetate molecule sits adjacent to the zinc ion, while 204 

a water molecule forms coordinating hydrogen bonds with the metal (Fig. 3D) . This specific 205 

arrangement remarkably mimics the nucleophilic attack on an ester bond, offering a structural 206 

basis for the preserved catalytic turnover observed in this variant. Further analysis revealed the 207 

structural origins of its poor substrate affinity (Fig. 3E) . The Zn (II)-coordinating histidine 208 

(H60) exhibited significant flexibility, wobbling away from the designed active site. In 209 

addition, rotamer shifts of other key histidine residues resulted in a steric clash with the 210 

coordinated zinc of the theozyme pose, which would thus lead to a steric clash between the 211 

substrate and the buried sidechains within this pocket. This lack of shape complementarity 212 

prevents effective substrate binding, consistent with the undetermined KM in kinetic assays 213 

(Fig. 3F) . In contrast, structural analysis of the high-affinity variant 11-7 revealed a precisely 214 

preorganized catalytic center even in the apo-state, which explains the measurable KM and high 215 

catalytic efficiency (Fig. 3 G-I) .216 

Activity improvement within sequence spaces 217 

Navigating across sequence landscapes, either naturally or through experimental 218 

approaches, has already proven effective in optimizing enzymes within evolutionary 219 

boundaries (38 , 39 ). However, the absence of evolutionary fitness information in de 220 

novo enzymes limits the efficacy of conventional optimization strategies, which typically rely 221 

on homologous sequence data to guide mutational trajectories ( 14 , 40 ). To explore the 222 

capability of IMUSE to navigate the sequence spaces of interest de novo enzymes, we selected 223

To tackle the observed kinetic trade-offs, we developed a predictive machine learning 243 

framework capable of rationally navigating sequence space by integrating both sequence and 244 

structural features. Recognizing that sequence alone is insufficient to capture the specific 245 

mechanistic bottlenecks of diverse de novo scaffolds, we fine-tuned the structure-aware protein 246 

language model, SaProt, using our dense mutational datasets (Fig. 4G and fig. S17A) (43 ). In 247 

this pipeline, coarse structural tokens extracted from the representative backbones (3-4 and 30-248 

3) via Foldseek were concatenated with mutation sequences to form comprehensive input 249 

representations ( 28 ). To counteract class imbalance within the high-throughput data and 250 

robustly distinguish highly functional variants, we employed a tailored batching strategy. By 251 

complementing the default cross-entropy loss with a supervised contrastive loss, the model 252 

effectively segregates the latent embeddings of distinct functional labels while clustering 253 

similar ones, thereby enhancing its resolution of the complex sequence-structure-function 254 

landscape. 255 

We rigorously evaluated this strategy through retrospective benchmarking (80/20 256 

training/test split), measuring the model's capacity to generate a precisely ranked output list of 257 

candidate enzymes. Model performance was quantified using Precision@ n%, Enrichment@ 258 

n%, and Recall@ n%, metrics that rigorously capture the fraction of true positive designs 259 

enriched at the very top of the prediction list. By effectively prioritizing favorable variants, our 260 

model enriched function-enhanced enzymes in the top 50 positions at a rate more than 50-fold 261 

higher than random sampling. Notably, this structure-informed strategy substantially 262 

outperformed predictions from the sequence-only ESM-2 model on this synthetic library ( Fig. 263 

4G and fig. S17 ) ( 27 ). Overall, our retrospective benchmarking validated the ability of IMUSE 264 

strategy to address the lack of evolutionary fitness information, providing an essential 265 

foundation for the effective sampling of higher-order multi-mutations. 266

Navigating expanded structural space for active enzymes 283 

Having established the capability for sequence space navigation, we proposed that this 284 

IMUSE could also be applied to navigate from a given structural space to an expanded space 285 

to distinguish potential active de novo enzymes. Previous retrospective analysis of Lib1 dataset 286 

revealed that none of the conventional metrics showed a significant correlation with 287 

experimental activity, highlighting the difficulty of evaluating de novo enzymes that lack 288 

evolutionary metadata ( 37 ). This failure of general scoring functions motivated us to develop 289 

a data-driven approach tailored to capture the complex fitness landscapes locally governing 290 

catalytic activity. We hypothesized that the functional data generated by IMUSE strategy could 291 

bridge this gap by learning features invisible to simple correlation models. To test this strategy, 292 

we adopted the broad genotype-phenotype data from the first-generation library (Lib1), 293 

establishing the first structural-level dataset of de novo enzyme–substrate complexes reported 294 

to date. Informed by the slight enrichment observed in AlphaFold3 confidence scores, we 295 

developed a geometric deep learning framework that integrates sequence embeddings from 296 

ESM2, residue-level pLDDT confidence scores from AlphaFold3, and structural 297 

representations extracted from design model ( Fig. 5A, fig. S21 ) ( 27 , 36 ). Specifically, we 298 

used AlphaFold3 to predict and incorporate residue-level pLDDT scores as node-level 299 

features, enabling the model to explicitly account for structural confidence in AF3 predictions 300 

(36 ). These features, together with 3D atomic coordinates and residue identities, were 301 

represented as graphs in which spatial relationships were encoded using radial basis functions. 302 

To integrate sequence and structural information, we fused sequence-level ESM2 303 

embeddings with structural node features via a cross-attention mechanism ( 27 ). Notably, for 304 

zinc-coordinated scaffolds, we introduced a dedicated attention module that prioritizes the 305 

catalytic environment, allowing the model to capture local coordination geometry and 306 

functionally dominant interactions. The overall architecture employs a GVP-GNN encoder to 307 

perform geometric message passing and generate graph-level embeddings for activity 308 

prediction. Finally, a classification head was used to predict binary outputs (True/False), which 309 

were further used to derive a ranking score. 310 

To validate the activity predictor, we applied benchmarking experiments to evaluate its 311 

overall effectiveness in enrichment of active enzymes from the initial library. In consideration 312 

of the low ratio of recovered designs in the Lib1, we implemented an 80/20 training/test split 313 

for this retrospectively analysis. This model was able to enrich the predictions ranked in the 314 

top fifty positions with enzymes with catalytic activity >2-fold better than would be observed 315

Next, we focused on these active designs for detailed characterization ( fig. S24 to S27 ). 340 

Strikingly, 16 of these 28 designs (~57%) exhibited measurable binding affinity and catalytic 341 

activity toward the substrate (Fig. 5E, fig. S26) , which stands in contrast to the 40% success 342 

rate typically observed in Lib1. Kinetic characterization further revealed that two of these 343 

model-selected designs displayed catalytic proficiency (-log Ktx ) exceeding the most active 344 

designs from the first library and even comparable to native enzyme (Fig. 5F, fig. S28 and 345 

table S3) (47 ). These results represent a marked success in the exploration of data-346 

impoverished structural space for de novo enzyme design, and they underscore that the lessons 347 

we have learned from high-throughput screening can be applied to generate a diverse and 348 

highly active set of designs suitable for either high- or low-throughput screening. 349 

Discussion 350 

Systematic exploration of the de novo structural landscape is a prerequisite for 351 

advancing biocatalysis, yet the absence of evolutionary metadata and landscape-activity 352 

complexity have historically limited the navigation of these vast spaces ( 21 , 37 , 38 , 48 ). 353 

IMUSE firstly address this conflict by effectively emulating the data-dense environment of 354 

natural evolution, generating the synthetic fitness landscapes required to bridge the gap 355 

between computational design and functional realization. Our approach provides a structural 356 

diversity that significantly exceeds the limitations of natural metagenomic libraries, which 357 

enables the interrogation of entirely novel landscapes, transcending the phylogenetic 358 

constraints of traditional enzyme engineering. Current methods for developing biocatalysts for 359 

unnatural substrate rely on remodeling from natural scaffolds or screening from de novo 360 

designs ( 17 ). Constrained by number of native folds and screening efficiency, these methods 361

Furthermore, we demonstrate that sdDE-FACS is an effective strategy for the high-366 

throughput isolation of active de novo enzymes by coupling genotype to phenotype, and that 367

(49 –51 ). The combination of de novo enzyme design and sdDE-FACS enabled the 369 

implementation of effective design-test-learn cycles, yielding a deeper understanding of the 370 

design principles for de novo enzymes as previously validated in de novo –designed 371 

miniproteins ( 52 , 53 ). This platform provided a foundational framework to accelerate the 372 

discovery of de novo biocatalysts across previously inaccessible regions of the protein universe 373 

and would advance the development of next-generation, AI-based enzyme design methods. 374
