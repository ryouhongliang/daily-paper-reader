Abstract 6

The interdomain flexibility of GT-B fold glycosyltransferases regulates substrate binding 7

and catalysis, yet the role of local structural variations in donor substrate specificity remains 8

unclear. GumK, a GT70 enzyme from Xanthomonas campestris , exhibits local plasticity 9

within its donor-binding pocket. We classify this plasticity into two conformational states, 10 

defined by the presence (closed state) or absence (open state) of a conserved hydrophobic 11 

interaction stabilizing the pocket. Using the AI-enhanced docking approach GNINA, we 12 

investigated the relationship between these states and substrate specificity by comparing 13 

UDP-glucuronate with five acidic and neutral ligand analogs. While docking scores showed 14 

limited discrimination among ligands, distance-based analysis between the sugar C6 atom 15 

and Lys307 revealed conformation-dependent trends. In the open state, negatively charged 16 

sugars preferentially interact with Lys307 via their carboxylate groups. Conversely, the closed 17 

state favors interactions with the pyrophosphate moiety. To provide orthogonal validation, 18 

we generated ensembles of GumK–substrate complexes using AI-based cofolding methods 19 

(AlphaFold3 and Boltz). While Boltz-2 affinities correctly ranked substrate specificity, only 20 

the GNINA-generated ensembles were consistent with physics-based simulations in capturing 21 

the conformational basis of selectivity. These results suggest that donor specificity arises 22 

from the interplay between ligand chemistry and binding-site plasticity rather than from a 23 

single rigid binding mode, and highlight complementary strengths of docking and cofolding 24 

approaches for studying flexible CAZymes. 25 

Keywords: GNINA, AI, glycosyltransferase, Xanthan gum, GumK, docking, cofolding models 26 

# Introduction 27 

Glycosyltransferases (GTs) catalyze the formation of glycosidic bonds and are essential for 28 

the biosynthesis of a wide range of glycoconjugates and polysaccharides. By transferring a 29 

monosaccharide unit from an activated donor, typically a nucleotide sugar, to a specific acceptor, 30 

these enzymes regulate the assembly of complex carbohydrate structures and their derivatives [1]. 31 

Due to their catalytic versatility, GTs are increasingly used as biocatalysts to produce glycans 32 

and functional biomaterials, and are also attractive targets for therapeutic intervention [2–4]. 33 

Despite their broad relevance, the molecular principles governing donor recognition and catalytic 34 

selectivity remain only partially understood. 35 

GTs display a high degree of structural diversity and are classified into 139 families in the 36 

CAZy database based on sequence similarity [5]. These families adopt different structural folds, 37 

including GT-A, GT-B, GT-C, and other less common architectures [6], which reflect the wide 38 

range of catalytic strategies and substrate specificities within the superfamily. 39 

Among the different architectures, the GT-B fold is one of the most abundant and conserved. 40 

GT-B enzymes are composed of two Rossmann-like domains connected by a flexible linker, with 41

The limited structural and mechanistic characterization of many GT-B enzymes poses a 48 

challenge for understanding their function and for exploiting them in biotechnological applications. 49 

In this context, the development of efficient strategies to rapidly screen donor–enzyme interactions 50 

would be highly valuable [9]. Computational approaches offer a promising starting point, providing 51 

atomistic insight into substrate recognition and enabling the exploration of multiple candidate 52 

substrates in a relatively high-throughput manner. 53 

Among the less explored GT-B families, GT70 includes only one characterized member, 54 

GumK, a GT from Xanthomonas campestris involved in the biosynthesis of xanthan gum, a highly 55 

versatile microbial polysaccharide widely used in the food and materials industries [10, 11]. GumK 56 

catalyzes the transfer of glucuronic acid from UDP-glucuronate to a lipid-linked oligosaccharide 57 

intermediate, thus incorporating an acidic sugar moiety that contributes to the physicochemical 58 

properties of the final polymer [12]. The crystal structure of GumK (PDB ID: 2HY7)[13] revealed 59 

Asp157 as the catalytic base, consistent with an inverting reaction mechanism [14]. It has 60 

been experimentally shown that GumK binds UDP-glucuronate, but neither UDP-glucose nor 61 

UDP-galacturonic acid [15]. 62 

Understanding donor selectivity is of particular interest for both mechanistic and biotech-63 

nological reasons. While UDP-glucuronate is the native substrate, incorporating alternative 64 

UDP-sugars could enable the biosynthesis of modified xanthan derivatives with altered charge 65 

distribution and mechanical properties. The discrimination between acidic and neutral sugar 66 

moieties is expected to arise from specific electrostatic interactions within the donor-binding 67 

pocket, yet the residues responsible for this selectivity have not been conclusively identified [13, 68 

15]. 69 

In a previous computational study [16], we investigated the conformational landscape of 70 

GumK and the conformational space of the monosaccharidic part of the donor substrate when 71 

the nucleotidic moiety is in the bound state at the isolated C-domain. We compared the ring 72 

orientation of UDP-glucuronate (the natural substrate) with that of the non-substrate UDP-73 

glucose. We observed that the polarity of the C-domain’s binding site, together with its flexibility, 74 

may influence donor selectivity by favoring interaction between the carboxyl group of glucuronate 75 

and the key residue Lys307. This interaction may be important for both the catalytic mechanism 76 

and the substrate-binding pathway, providing an additional enthalpic contribution that is absent 77 

in UDP-glucose. 78 

However, the computational strategy used to sample sugar conformations was not high-79 

throughput, thereby limiting the utility of the approach for studying multiple donor substrate 80 

candidates or for screening mutants with potentially altered activity. To address this sampling 81 

limitation, we herein explore the use of docking as a complementary approach to investigate 82 

the interactions of various ligands with both full-length GumK and its isolated C-domain. We 83 

performed systematic docking calculations using GNINA [17], an AI-based scoring algorithm that 84 

has demonstrated improved performance over the standard AutoDock Vina scoring scheme. Our 85 

results show that the docking algorithm recovers pose populations similar to those generated by 86 

molecular dynamics (MD) simulations [16], enabling discrimination of substrates based on geo-87 

metric descriptors. However, we found the outcome is highly sensitive to the conformation of the 88 

donor-binding site within the C-domain of GumK. Thanks to recent developments in AI-based co-89 

folding methods that enable the prediction of protein–ligand complexes, we additionally employed 90 

AlphaFold3 and two versions of Boltz to generate multiple structural samples for each UDP-sugar 91 

ligand in complex with GumK. This comparison allows us to assess whether independent cofolding 92

# Results and Discussion 100 

Conformational states of the donor-binding site 101 

We focused our study on the donor-binding domain (C-domain) of GumK to isolate its binding 102 

properties from interactions with the acceptor domain. This choice also simplifies the highly 103 

dynamic two-domain enzyme into a relatively static one, thereby avoiding the need to sample 104 

multiple states with varying degrees of opening when studying the donor substrate’s conformation 105 

in the binding site [16]. 106 

The donor-binding domain of GumK shows a peculiar characteristic that is absent in the 107 

other xanthan gum GT-Bs, namely a flexible loop between residues 293–300, which shows high 108 

variability in terms of amino acid composition within the CAZy family of GumK (GT70) [5, 16]. 109 

This loop is not directly in contact with the donor substrate, as a hydrophobic interaction between 110 

Met231 and Leu301 positions it away from the donor-binding site. The crystal structures of 111 

GumK in the apo form (PDB: 2HY7) (Fig. 1A) and in complex with UDP (PDB: 2Q6V) (Fig. 1B) 112 

reveal that these hydrophobic residues adopt different conformational states. One conformation 113 

shows the side chain of Met231 interacting with that of Leu301 in the apo structure, whereas this 114 

interaction is absent in the UDP-bound complex. These residues are conserved within the GT70 115 

family, and their conformational plasticity is partially attributable to the aforementioned flexible 116 

loop. 117 

Patrick and colleagues used NMR spectroscopy to show that binding of the donor substrate 118 

is initially guided by the nucleobase, i.e., UDP, in a glycosyltransferase from E. coli (WaaG) 119 

that binds UDP-glucose [18]. Once this part is bound, the unconstrained sugar moiety retains 120 

conformational freedom, which is lost as the substrate approaches the catalytic pocket and finally 121 

binds. 122 

Considering the conformational flexibility of both the binding site and the donor substrate, we 123 

evaluated the performance of GNINA in docking the native substrate and a series of UDP-sugars 124 

that are present in the metabolism of Xanthomonas as building blocks for several glycosylation 125 

reactions: UDP-glucose, UDP-galactose, UDP-galacturonate, UDP-N -acetylglucosamine, and 126 

UDP-N -acetylgalactosamine. Each ligand was docked onto two different states of the donor 127 

domain: one conformation obtained from the prediction of the C-domain in complex with full-128 

length UDP-glucuronate using Boltz-2 (named conf0 in this work)[19] (Fig. 1C), and a second 129 

conformation extracted from a previously published [16] unbiased MD simulation of the isolated 130 

donor domain (named conf1 in this work) (Fig. 1D). 131 

The conf1 corresponds to an open state of the hydrophobic interaction, confirming that this 132 

is a natural behavior of the donor-binding site and does not depend on the presence of the 133 

acceptor-binding domain, as suggested by the crystal structure [13]. In contrast, the Boltz2 134 

cofolded complex, conf0, displays a closed state of the hydrophobic interaction. In this model, 135 

the sugar moiety does not directly interact with the binding domain, and binding is primarily 136 

mediated by the pyrophosphate group. 137

GNINA docking recapitulates MD-derived pose populations 138 

For each ligand, a GNINA docking approach was employed to obtain a statistically meaningful 139 

representation of the accessible binding poses. We ran 31 replicas per ligand, with each replica 140 

generating 20 poses, yielding a total of 31 ×20 docked poses per ligand. We plotted the distributions 141 

of the median GNINA CNN score (convolutional neural network score) computed for each replica, 142 

i.e., over 20 poses (Fig. 2A and B). We filtered out poses in which the nucleobase was not in the 143 

crystallographic binding mode, based on RMSD relative to the crystal structure (PDB 2Q6V). 144 

For the remaining poses, we computed a bootstrapped distribution of the distance between the 145 

C6 atom of the ligand and the N η atom of the key residue Lys307 ( dK ) (Fig. 2C and D). 146 

Inspection of the distributions of median CNN scores across ligands reveals no apparent 147 

difference. The only distribution that deviates from the native substrate (UDP-GlcA) is UDP-148 

GlcNAc in the conf0 state of the binding site. Assuming that the donor domain specifically binds 149 

UDP-GlcA, then the score alone cannot be used to discriminate between substrates, as it fails to 150 

distinguish between a good binder and a non-binder. 151

The GNINA-generated distributions do not completely overlap with those generated by MD 170

In MD simulations, the sampled function corresponds to a biased potential energy surface 174 

(using Hamiltonian Replica Exchange [16]) defined by a force field that models the physical 175 

interactions of the system. In contrast, GNINA employs a CNN-based scoring function in which 176 

atomic positions within the docking box are associated with the likelihood of observing that 177 

distribution in experimental crystal structures. Specifically, the CNN predicts both a pose score 178 

(a confidence score between 0 and 1 that a pose has a low RMSD relative to the experimental 179 

structure) and binding affinity in pK units [20]. The scoring function, therefore, contains no 180 

explicit physical energy terms. 181 

Although the CNN score could not discriminate among substrates, it provided useful insights 182 

into binding-site selectivity. As the protein transitions from the open (conf1) to closed (conf0) 183 

conformation, the median CNN score distributions become less overlapping (Fig. 2A–B). However, 184 

due to system flexibility and multiple local minima, poses with an improperly bound nucleobase 185 

can still achieve high CNN scores and vice versa, since the nucleobase’s binding contribution can 186 

be partially compensated by the sugar–pyrophosphate moiety. By introducing an RMSD filter and 187 

a suitable binding-mode descriptor, GNINA nonetheless recovered a conformation distribution 188 

resembling that of a physical potential energy surface. This likely reflects the CNN’s complex 189 

representation of the chemical environment, though extracting interpretable information for a 190 

specific docked system remains challenging [21]. 191 

Carbohydrates are cyclic molecules whose ring structures impose important conformational 192 

constraints. A potential limitation of scoring functions that are not explicitly physics-based 193 

is that they may generate highly distorted ligand conformations or fail to preserve the correct 194 

stereochemistry. Consequently, structural filtering is often required to identify and remove 195 

physically unrealistic poses [22]. However, for GNINA, we did not observe significant distortions, 196 

suggesting that the model is reasonably well-suited to these types of molecules. The limited 197 

quantitative discrimination observed for the CNN score may be related to the nature of the 198 

training dataset. Structural information for GT-B glycosyltransferases remains relatively scarce, 199 

and GumK is currently the only member of the CAZy GT70 family for which an experimental 200 

structure is available. As a result, binding modes and structural features specific to this enzyme 201 

family are likely underrepresented in the data used to train the scoring model, potentially affecting 202 

its performance on these systems [23]. 203 

Despite these limitations, we demonstrated that prior knowledge from more computationally 204 

demanding physics-based simulations, combined with a discriminating variable to characterize 205 

the generated conformations, yielded an AI-based docking approach that provides meaningful 206 

insights into the behavior of these systems while retaining the advantages of high-throughput 207 

screening. 208 

Cofolding predicts substrate affinity but not conformational specificity 209 

To provide orthogonal validation of the docking-based analysis, we generated ensembles of 210 

GumK–substrate complexes using AlphaFold3 [24] and Boltz-2 [19] by producing 200 diffusion 211 

samples per UDP-sugar substrate. This approach was motivated by the hypothesis that a 212 

sufficiently large number of samples could capture alternative interdomain conformations, yielding 213 

a receptor ensemble implicitly coupled to each substrate. 214

GNINA docking on full-length GumK shows the effect of the N-domain 243 

The docking experiments on the isolated C-domain enabled us to isolate the effect of the binding-244 

site conformation on donor-substrate binding and potential substrate selectivity. However, the 245 

binding site of the isolated C-domain is largely solvent-exposed, allowing a greater conformational 246 

freedom for the sugar moiety. In contrast, the presence of the N-domain in the closed conformation 247 

may contribute to substrate selectivity by further constraining the orientation of the ligand. To 248 

test this hypothesis, we generated full-length GumK–ligand complexes by cofolding UDP-GlcA 249 

and UDP-GalA with Boltz-1 and subsequently performed docking calculations to evaluate how 250 

the N-domain affects the GNINA-generated distributions (Fig. 4). 251 

The cofolded structures adopt a closed-state conformation, in which the pyrophosphate region 252 

of the ligand interacts with Lys307. Based on the Met231–Leu301 distance, the resulting structures 253 

correspond to the conf0 state of the C-domain. 254 

Redocking with GNINA, using the same protocol as for the isolated C-domain, revealed subtle 255 

but noticeable differences in the distance distributions. In particular, the interaction between 256 

the carboxyl group and Lys307 is less populated in the case of UDP-GlcA (Fig. 4A), and both 257 

ligands display a dominant peak around 8 Å, consistent with interactions between Lys307 and 258 

the pyrophosphate moiety. This shift in the distribution may be explained by the additional 259 

contacts provided by the N-domain, which, as expected, further restricts the orientation of the 260 

ligand within the binding site. 261 

Ensemble docking considerations 262 

Although the lack of receptor flexibility represents an intrinsic limitation of the docking approach, 263 

it may also provide a conceptual advantage. By constraining the protein conformation, docking 264 

enables a more controlled comparison of how predefined binding-site states influence the spatial 265 

distribution of the docked ligands, thereby facilitating the dissection of conformation-dependent 266 

specificity. 267 

The docking study suggests that acidic ligands contribute an additional enthalpic component 268 

to binding through interactions between the carboxylate and Lys307. This interaction may 269 

influence the binding pathway by properly orienting the substrate for catalysis. The flexibility 270 

of the donor-binding site may guide this interaction through two steps: in a more open state, 271 

the sugar moiety can interact with Lys307 via its carboxyl group, as there is sufficient space to 272 

accommodate the sugar ring in the proper orientation. When the Met-Leu loop adopts a closed 273 

conformation, the interaction with the carboxyl group is reduced, whereas interactions with the 274 

pyrophosphate group become more favored, allowing the sugar ring to approach the catalytic 275 

residue in the acceptor-binding domain (Fig. 5). 276

# Conclusion 290 

In this study, we explored the potential of GNINA to investigate donor recognition in a system 291 

characterized by substantial conformational flexibility at both the binding site and the substrate 292 

level. GumK presents a challenging case for rigid docking approaches, as the donor-binding 293 

domain can adopt multiple conformations, and the UDP-sugar retains residual flexibility even 294 

after nucleobase anchoring. 295 

To account for this structural variability, we represented the conformational heterogeneity of 296 

the donor-binding site through two structurally defined states. These were operationally defined 297 

based on the presence or absence of a conserved hydrophobic interaction that stabilizes the pocket: 298 

an open state, in which this interaction is disrupted, and a closed state, in which it is maintained. 299 

By docking multiple UDP-sugars to these two predefined conformations, we aimed to assess 300 

whether GNINA could capture substrate-dependent trends across distinct structural contexts. 301 

While the median CNN score does not clearly discriminate between substrates, analysis 302 

of geometric descriptors suggests that substrate behavior depends on the binding pocket’s 303 

conformational state. In the closed state (conf0; Fig. 5A), the contacts between pyrophosphate 304 

and Lys307 represent the main population. In contrast, in the open state (conf1; Fig. 5B), acidic 305 

sugars more frequently adopt conformations compatible with an interaction between the carboxyl 306 

group and Lys307. 307 

A similar picture emerges for the full-length protein, where the constraints on the conforma-308 

tional freedom of the sugar ligand arise not only from the architecture of the binding site within a 309 

single domain, but also from the presence of the second domain. This effect can be qualitatively 310 

appreciated from the changes observed in the distributions of the geometric descriptor used to 311 

characterize the sampled binding modes. 312

Furthermore, we compared the AI-based docking approach with the cofolding methods 319 

implemented in AlphaFold3 and Boltz. In contrast to docking algorithms, where AI is primarily 320 

used to score conformations generated by a sampling procedure, cofolding methods rely on the 321 

AI model itself to directly sample the conformational space of the protein–ligand complex. In our 322 

case, these models appear to be biased toward a very narrow binding mode, likely reflecting the 323 

limited amount of experimental structural information available for such systems. In addition, 324 

they occasionally fail to preserve the correct stereochemistry of the ligands. 325 

Despite not being explicitly physics-based, GNINA is surprisingly capable of generating an 326 

ensemble of binding modes that resembles those sampled during molecular dynamics simulations. 327 

This highlights the value of combining AI models with knowledge obtained from more computa-328 

tionally demanding methods and suggests that there is still considerable room for improvement 329 

in current cofolding approaches. Importantly, our results show that simple affinity scores alone 330 

are insufficient to discriminate between favorable and unfavorable ligands. Instead, meaningful 331 

interpretation requires analysis of the generated conformations using appropriate geometric 332 

descriptors. Importantly, due to its computational efficiency, the GNINA-based approach used 333 

in this work could, in principle, be extended to rapid screening of mutants, provided that the 334 

conformational ensemble of the donor-binding site is adequately represented. In this context, 335 

docking would not replace detailed molecular simulations, but could serve as an initial filter 336 

to identify variants whose binding-site conformations preserve or alter key substrate-dependent 337 

interactions. 338 

Overall, this work suggests that AI-based docking, combined with an explicit representation 339 

of the binding site’s open and closed states, may provide a practical, exploratory framework for 340 

generating testable hypotheses about donor selectivity in flexible GT-B enzymes. 341 

# Methods 342 

GNINA 343 

The first conformation of the C-domain (conf0) was predicted using Boltz-2 [19], which takes 344 

as input only the sequence of the isolated domain and the CCD definition of UDP-GlcA (see 345 

below). The top-ranked model was selected and used as a receptor for docking. conf1 was instead 346 

extracted from a preparative MD simulation of the C-domain in solution. 347 

Each ligand was docked using GNINA (version 1.3) [17]. The docking search space was 348 

defined by an autobox centered on residues surrounding the donor-binding site (228–231, 253– 349 

254, 271–275, 278, 292, 301, 304–308, and 310), with automatic box extension enabled. The 350 

exhaustiveness parameter was set to 16, and the minimum RMSD between poses during the 351 

Monte Carlo sampling was set to 1 Å to filter redundant conformations. For each ligand, 31 352 

independent docking runs were performed, each initialized with a different random seed and 353 

generating 20 poses per replica. 354 

Since both receptor conformations (conf0 and conf1) were structurally aligned to the C-domain 355 

domain of the crystal structure (PDB ID: 2Q6V), the RMSD of the uridine moiety with respect 356 

to the crystallographic reference was used as a geometric filter. Among the poses generated for 357 

each replica, only those with a CNN score above the median and with the uridine moiety correctly 358 

positioned within the binding site were retained for further analysis. 359 

Cofolding methods 360 

AlphaFold3 (v3.0.1) [24], Boltz-1 (v1.0.0) [27], and Boltz-2 (v2.2.1) [19] were used to predict 361 

cofolded structures of conf0 (see above) with each of the six UDP-sugar ligands, identified by 362

Full-legth Gumk docking 367 

The full-length GumK complexes with UDP-GlcA and UDP-GalA were predicted using Boltz-1 368 

(v1.0.0), employing the CCD codes UGA and UGB for the respective ligands. Three diffusion 369 

samples were generated for each complex, although only the top-ranked model was selected for 370 

the docking analysis. After removing the cofolded ligand, GNINA docking calculations were 371 

performed using the same protocol adopted for the isolated C-domain. The resulting poses were 372 

filtered based on the uridine binding mode by calculating the RMSD of the uridine moiety relative 373 

to the crystallographic reference structure (PDB ID: 2Q6V). Only poses reproducing the reference 374 

uridine conformation were retained for subsequent analysis. 375 

Distance distribution analysis 376 

For the filtered poses generated by the GNINA workflow and the conformational ensembles 377 

produced by the cofolding methods, the distance between the C6 atom of the ligand and the 378 

ζ-nitrogen of Lys307 was measured. Kernel density estimation (KDE) was then computed to 379 

characterize the distribution of this distance. To assess variability in the sampled conformations, 380 

a bootstrapped KDE was computed using 4000 resampling cycles, and the standard deviation 381 

was estimated from the resulting distribution. 382
